---
title: 'A Slow Conversation'
description: 'How a habit of filling notebook margins became a generative art project.'
pubDate: 2026-05-27
---

It started in the margins.

Somewhere in the first year of undergrad, in the white space beside my lecture notes, a small shape appeared. Then another. I'd outline something: a pattern, an abstract knot, my own name stretched into a font I'd invented that morning and then I'd begin to fill it in. A little hatching here. A field of dots there. Section by section, until the empty shape was dense with pattern and the lecture had moved on without me.

I never decided to do this. I can't point to the day it began. But by the time those four years were over, the margins of every notebook I owned were crowded edge to edge, and the filling-in had become something my hand did on its own, in the in-betweens, while the rest of me was somewhere else. An unconscious habit. A quiet conversation between my hand and the paper that I was barely even present for.

Years later, when I went looking for a way into generative art, that habit was the first thing that surfaced.

---

What pulls most people toward generative art is a kind of surrender. You don't make the image, instead you write the rules, step back, and let the code make it for you. The artist becomes a gardener: you plant the conditions and watch what grows.

I loved that idea. But I didn't want to step all the way back. I wanted the work to be code-generated and still somehow *mine*. I wanted it to carry the fingerprint of a hand. So I built the thing backwards from how these pieces usually go. Not a system that runs on its own, but one that waits for me.

You draw. It draws with you.

That was the whole idea, and everything else was just trying to be worthy of it.

---

I called it *A Slow Conversation*.

You make a mark, and you pause. In that pause, the piece studies what you've left behind. It goes looking for the shapes your lines have closed, the loops you've sealed without quite meaning to. And then, quietly, it fills them. A held breath, an answer, and then it waits again for you to speak.

The way it finds those closed shapes is the trick I'm fondest of, because it's so simple it feels like cheating. The piece floods the canvas inward from all four corners. Imagine pouring water around the outside of your drawing and watching it seep into every open space. Wherever the water *can't* reach is, by definition, somewhere you've walled off. Those dry, sealed pockets are your shapes. The drawing tells the machine where its enclosures are simply by being drawn.

---

It did not arrive looking like this.

The first version knew only one language: **tangles**, the repeating pen-patterns from my margins, hatching and scales and little rows of eyes. Then came **Tones**, where a shape fills not with line but with quiet bands of a single colour, light bleeding into dark like a papercut held up to a window.

And then I reached for something I wasn't ready to hold.

I'm Indian, and I wanted the piece to speak in the art I'd grown up inside such as Madhubani, Warli, the folk traditions whose lines I'd known long before I knew the word *generative*. I spent real time teaching the code their motifs. And the code learned them, in the way a parrot learns a sentence. The shapes came out correct and completely lifeless. Mechanical. The forms were there and the feeling had drained out of them entirely, and what was left didn't just look wrong. It felt like a disservice to the originals, to the hands and the histories those traditions carry.

So I deleted all of it.

It turned out to be the most useful thing that happened. The failure showed me where the edge of my own idea was. Madhubani and Warli are human to the root; their soul was never a thing I could ask a machine to generate, because the soul is the part that comes from a person. What code is actually good at is something cooler and older: geometry, symmetry, the patience of perfect repetition. So I stopped asking it to feel, and asked it instead to build.

That became **Mandalas**: circular ornaments grown from nothing: a border, a stack of patterned rings, a still centre, packed together inside a shape like stones fitted into a wall. Here, the machine's way of working isn't a poor imitation of a human. It's its own kind of beautiful.

Three boards in the end. Tones, Tangles, Mandalas. Three answers to one question *What does it mean to fill an empty shape?* which, I realised somewhere along the way, was the only question I'd ever been asking in those margins.

---

If I'm proud of one thing, it's this: the piece never repeats itself. The piece remembers. It keeps a record of every single ornament it has ever drawn in a sitting, and if it's about to repeat one, it quietly rolls again.

Every tone, tangle and mandala is assembled fresh: a border, or a color, or a zentangle chosen at random, a color chosen at random, rings drawn from a deep vocabulary of motifs, a centre to hold them.

There's something in that I can't quite let go of. A small machine, generating and generating, refusing to ever say the same thing twice.

---

If you'd like to talk to it: draw a slow loop, and pause. Watch what it gives back.

It owes its grammar to the traditional practice of zentangles and mandalas. But mostly it owes itself to a notebook margin, in a lecture I wasn't paying attention to, where a hand I wasn't watching taught itself to fill the empty spaces.
