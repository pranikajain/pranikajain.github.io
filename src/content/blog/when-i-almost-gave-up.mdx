---
title: 'When I almost gave up'
description: 'A first hike, ten years on.'
pubDate: 2026-05-23
---

I went on a hike today, the kind of small civilized hike where there's a parking lot with a restroom at the start, and somewhere along the trail my brain decided, without my permission, to play back the memory of my first hike. The memory came back with more weight than I had asked it to.

I have not thought about Bhimashankar in some time. My first hike was almost a decade ago, in monsoon, in the Sahyadris near Pune. Today's walk brought it back, and the memory has been sitting with me ever since. Which is how I have ended up here, writing this.

The trek happened in September 2017. I went with the Hyderabad Trekking Club, which is a sentence that sounds, in retrospect, like I knew what I was signing up for. I did not. The club had advertised a two-day hike. I had read the words two-day hike and concluded that two days was a reasonable amount of time to walk up a hill. We started in the afternoon. We climbed through a wet forest up a route called the Shidi Ghat. Shidi, I would later learn, means ladder.

It rained heavily, the way only monsoon in the Sahyadris rains. The kind of rain that gets under your raincoat as a matter of policy. The kind of rain that makes your shoes a separate weather system on each foot. We climbed in it all the way up.

By the time we reached the family's house where we were going to sleep, I was so wrung out, in the body and somewhere beneath the body. The house was just one large hall, lit by one electric bulb hanging from the ceiling, where all of us would lay out our sleeping bags for the night like very tired sardines. I sat in the corner on top of my sleeping bag and typed this in the notes app.

---

<p><em>
Today, here I am amidst nature's green carpet,<br />
and this hell of a journey is not over yet.<br />
The journey was not about a silly traffic jam,<br />
not even close to "your cab is waiting at your doorstep, ma'am."
</em></p>

<p><em>
Today was about discovering a new you,<br />
the one lost in the race, lost somewhere within you too.<br />
It started with "no, I can't do it, let's go back."<br />
Who knew life is more beautiful when it's off track?
</em></p>

<p><em>
Today, I wonder what if I'd surrendered, said "I am giving up,"<br />
how empty I'd have left life's adventure cup.<br />
I trusted rocks that themselves were unsure,<br />
and they held me. People, I wish, could be that pure.
</em></p>

<p><em>
Today, the ladder, awaiting beauty on the other side, seemed hard to ascend.<br />
Open your eyes; life follows the same trend.<br />
I was where I was, away from the screen that makes the world small,<br />
not man to man, but nature to man, was the attended call.
</em></p>

<p><em>
Today, the strangers showed me they cared,<br />
while those who should have, somehow, hadn't dared.<br />
The supper was not just served, but a royal feast made of the ordinary,<br />
wrapped in warmth and a hospitality the world has long forgotten to carry.
</em></p>

<p><em>
Today, I felt the sun, the rain, the mountains, the power they uphold,<br />
and I went from bold, to coward, to bold.<br />
The life I'd taken for granted feels a little different.<br />
The scared little heart finally has the relief of an achievement.
</em></p>

Looking back, there were rocks on the Shidi Ghat that moved when I put my weight on them, and held anyway. There were ladders in the rain, three of them, bolted into the wet rock at angles that asked you to trust both the bolts and yourself. There was a meal that night at the family's house that I still remember as one of the best I've ever eaten, mostly because of the warmth of the people serving it.

Somewhere in the middle of the climb, I told the group I couldn't do it. I said I wanted to go back. I said it more than once. I remember the exact tone in which I said it, the deliberately calm tone of a person trying to sound reasonable while internally calculating how to be carried down a mountain. The group did not let me go back. They kept walking, and they kept telling me to think of how proud I would be of myself at the top, once I had done it. I am not sure I believed them in the moment. But obviously, they were right.

"From bold, to coward, to bold", those three words have come back to me more times than I can count, in years that had nothing to do with mountains. The shape of them is the shape of most things worth doing, I have come to think.

To many more first timers ahead.
